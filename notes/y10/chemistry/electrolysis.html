Electrolysis

Definitions

  • Electrolysis: electron current is passed through a compound to separate them
  • Electrolyte: compound that is to be electrolyzed
  • Electrodes: conducts electricity to separate the electrolyte

Setup

Note

Electrolysis only works where there are compounds with elements that form ions of opposite charge

Electrolyte

  • Electrolyte must be a liquid, that is because it needs to conduct electricity
  • Ionic compounds can be dissolved in water, where the ions are free to move around to carry charge (conduct electricity)
  • They can also be melted so the ionic bonds are broken and there will just be ions floating around

Electrodes

  • Electrodes are usually made of metal or graphite that are unreactive (inert)
  • Anode: positive
  • Cathode: negative

Process

  1. The ions are free to move around
  2. When the current passes, ions are attracted to the electrode with the opposite charge
  3. Positive ions are ended at the cathode (negative) and negative ions are ended at the anode (positive)
  4. At the electrodes the ion that is less reactive will be producted
  5. If the product is a solid, it will be deposited below the electrodes, if the product is a gas, it will escape as bubbles

Reactivity series

More notes

  • Hydrogen(H) forms a positive ion
  • Hydroxide(OH) forms a negative ion
  • If the element is from group 7 (Br, I, Cl, etc), it will always have pirority over the other elements when at electrodes
  • Gases are always in twos (H₂, O₂, etc)
  • 4OH -> 2H₂O + O₂
  • Upside down test tubes filled with on the electrolyte (liquid) can be used to collect gases
