Digital Cameras

To capture an image, a digital camera has millions of photosites. During exposure these photosites recives photons and charge build up. At the end of the exposure this charge can be used to determin the brightness of the pixel.

To create a coloured image, a Bayer mosaic is placed over the sensors, each filter has its own colour of red, green or blue, and each covers one photosites. This allows the colour intensity of each pixel to be determined.

To reflect the way a human eye is more sensitive to green light, the Bayer mosaic consist of twice as many green filters than other colours.

Film Cameras

The camera film contains silver-halide crystals, these crystals act as photon detectors, although they also react to other kinds of radiation. The silver-halide grains are mixed with sliver-nitrate and halide salts to create different sizes, shapes and compositions, this will change their surface area and light sensitivity.

Unmodified grains are only sensitive to blue lights (like how photosites are not colour sensitive), orgainic molecules known as spectral sensitizers are added to make the grains more sensitive to a specific colour.

Eye

The eye has 2 lenses, the un-squeezable cornea for coarse focusing and the "lens" for finer focusing. Muscles around the lens contracts to thicken it, reducing the focal length, allowing closer objects to be in focus. The pupil contracts and relaxes to control the amount of light to let it, preventing high light intensity from damaging the cells.

There are millions of rod and cone cells on the retina, where rod cells are responsible for monochromatic (black and white) and cone cells are responsible for colour vision. Rod cells also works better in low light environment, loss of rod cells will cause night blindness.

Similarties

Both cameras and eyes form real image that is inverted, the orientation is then corrected by the brain or some sort of software.

Also, both eyes and cameras use electric pulses to transfer information recieved for processing.

Differences

Photosites and grains for digital and film cameras are responsible for both brightness and colour (with Bayer mosaic filters), whereas rod and cone cells are responsible for (mainly) brightness and colour vision respectively.

To keep to image in-focus, eyes has a stretchy lens, the thickness and curvature changes as it is pulled, (hopefully) creating a focused image on the retina. Cameras move the sensors or the lens back and forth to get a focused image on the sensors or the film.

The human eye is very well designed, it has no depthh distortion, has an epic dynamic range as the cells are dynamically adjusted to the environment (mainly brightness). The human eye is also 24 times higher in resolution than the average cameras.

However, the human eye only sees the centre 20 degrees in detail, and has a blind spot caused by the exiting nerve, all which a camera does not have. Cemeras can also have wide or telephoto lens (like in telescopes), and multiple of these lens to fit a specific purpose, which the human eye only have one coarse and one fine lens.
